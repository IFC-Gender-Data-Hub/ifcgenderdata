From Participation to Quality Jobs

Women’s economic participation has improved globally, yet the nature of work remains unequal. Women are more likely than men to be concentrated in lower-quality jobs—those with lower pay, limited security, and fewer advancement opportunities (World Bank 2012; ILO 2024). While governments shape laws and institutions, firms play a decisive role in determining how opportunities are distributed in practice. Understanding how labor markets, legal frameworks, and corporate behavior interact is essential to closing the gap between participation and quality employment.


April 2026 — Tosin Gbadegesin

How Firms Shape Women’s Economic Opportunities

Women are working more than ever before. Across the world, female labor force participation has increased over time, supported by gains in education, declining fertility, and structural economic transformation (Goldin 1995; World Bank 2012). But working does not always mean working in quality jobs.

Quality jobs are defined as employment that provides stable earnings, social protection, safe working conditions, and opportunities for skill development and career advancement (World Bank 2012; ILO 2024). In many economies, women remain concentrated in forms of work that fall short of these standards, including own-account work and unpaid family work, which often lack income security and formal protections (ILO 2024; Fields 2011).

The result is a persistent gap between having a job and having a good job—and closing this gap increasingly depends on firm-level decisions.

A World of Unequal Job Quality

Data show that the share of women in vulnerable employment declined from about 44 percent in the early 1990s to around 37 percent in 2025. However, this progress masks large regional disparities. In Africa and South Asia, more than 60 percent of working women remain in vulnerable employment, while in Europe the share is closer to 13–14 percent.

These patterns—derived from the data—align with broader evidence linking higher informality and lower economic development to greater reliance on vulnerable employment (ILO 2024; World Bank 2019).

Some regions have made progress, yet many women remain concentrated in work that offers limited income security and few pathways for advancement. The challenge is not only job creation—but job quality.

Key Insight Women’s participation has increased globally, but job quality remains highly unequal across regions.

The Rules That Shape Opportunity

Equal pay for work of equal value is a core protection. While many countries have adopted such legislation, coverage remains uneven.

Evidence shows that stronger legal frameworks supporting women’s economic participation are associated with higher labor force participation and improved economic outcomes (World Bank 2024; Cuberes and Teignier 2016; IMF 2018).

The chart reflects where these laws exist; the literature explains why they matter. Where legal protections are limited, women may face persistent wage disparities—even in formal employment.

Why It Matters Legal equality expands opportunity—but only when firms translate laws into practice.

Inside Firms: Where Women Work

The data show wide variation in women’s participation in production roles and overall employment. In some countries, women represent a majority of workers; in others, they remain underrepresented.

These patterns reflect well-documented gender segregation across occupations and sectors (ILO 2024; World Bank 2012), while the magnitudes are drawn directly from firm-level data.

Production roles often provide entry points into stable employment. Where women are underrepresented, access to these opportunities is limited.

Key Insight Hiring patterns within firms shape women’s access to stable employment.

From Transparency to Accountability

The data show that 45.3 percent of firms publish gender-disaggregated pay information, but only 14.4 percent have strategies to close gender pay gaps.

Research suggests that transparency improves accountability (Bennedsen et al. 2022; World Bank 2024), yet few firms translate disclosure into action.

Data Gap Many firms measure gender pay gaps, but far fewer act to close them.

Building Pathways to Better Jobs

Most firms report policies ensuring equal access to training and career development.

Evidence links training to higher productivity and earnings (Becker 1993; ILO 2024). However, access is not always equal in practice.

Why It Matters Training supports upward mobility—but only if women can access it equally.
