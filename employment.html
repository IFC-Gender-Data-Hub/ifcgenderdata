From Jobs to Quality Jobs

Linking vulnerable employment, pay equity laws, and corporate practices

Women’s participation in labor markets has expanded globally, yet access to employment has not always translated into access to quality jobs. Many women remain concentrated in vulnerable forms of employment—where income stability, social protection, and opportunities for advancement are limited. This analysis explores how labor market structures, legal frameworks, and corporate practices shape equitable employment outcomes.

Persistent gaps in vulnerable employment

Globally, about 37 percent of women were engaged in vulnerable employment in 2025, compared with about 44 percent in the early 1990s, indicating gradual progress over time. Although global levels have declined, large regional differences remain.

In 2025, vulnerable employment among women remains above 60 percent in both Africa and South Asia, while it stands at roughly 13–14 percent in Europe. These disparities reflect differences in labor market structures and access to formal wage employment.

Despite improvements, many women remain concentrated in employment arrangements that lack stability and predictable earnings, underscoring the need for transitions into wage and salaried work.

Source: ILO Modelled Estimates Database (ILOEST), ILO. World Bank Gender Data Portal.

Legal frameworks shape economic opportunity

As women move into wage employment, legal protections become critical for ensuring that job quality includes fair compensation and equal economic opportunity. While many economies have adopted equal pay legislation, protections remain uneven across regions.

Where legal protections are limited, corporate policies become especially important in advancing pay equity in practice.

Source: Women, Business and the Law 2024.

Gender gaps persist in corporate leadership

Globally, women hold 28.6 percent of board seats and 26.7 percent of senior management positions. Regional variation remains significant, with Europe leading and the Middle East lagging behind.

Leadership diversity shapes corporate priorities and influences the adoption of gender-equitable workplace policies.

Transparency without action

Globally, 45.3 percent of firms disclose gender pay data, yet only 14.4 percent publish formal strategies to close pay gaps.

The gap between disclosure and implementation highlights the need for stronger accountability mechanisms.

Training policies and upward mobility

Access to training and career development plays a critical role in enabling women to transition into higher-quality jobs.

High adoption rates suggest growing recognition that inclusive training policies support workforce mobility.

Aligning labor markets, law, and corporate practice

Women’s employment outcomes are shaped by three interconnected forces: labor market structures, legal protections, and firm-level practices.

Expanding stable employment, strengthening equal pay laws, and promoting inclusive corporate policies are all essential to ensuring that labor force participation translates into equitable economic opportunities.
