From Jobs to Quality Jobs: Linking Vulnerable Employment, Pay Equity Laws, and Corporate Practices

Women’s participation in labor markets has expanded globally, yet access to employment has not always translated into access to quality jobs. Many women remain concentrated in vulnerable forms of employment such as self-employment, own-account work, or contributing family work—where income stability, social protection, and opportunities for advancement are limited (World Bank 2012; ILO 2024). This analysis examines how labor market structures, equal pay laws, and corporate policies shape whether women’s employment leads to equitable outcomes.

Persistent Gaps in Vulnerable Employment

Globally, about 37 percent of women were engaged in vulnerable employment in 2025, compared with about 44 percent in the early 1990s, indicating gradual progress over time. Although global levels have declined, large regional differences remain. In 2025, vulnerable employment among women remains above 60 percent in both Africa and South Asia, while it stands at roughly 13–14 percent in Europe.

Source: ILOEST, ILO. World Bank Gender Data Portal.

Legal Frameworks for Equal Pay and Economic Opportunity

Legal protections remain uneven across countries. Where protections are limited, women may face structural wage disparities even in formal employment.

Gender Gaps Persist in Corporate Leadership

Globally, women hold 28.6 percent of board seats and 26.7 percent of senior management positions, with large regional variation.

Transparency and Action on Gender Pay Gaps

While 45.3 percent of firms disclose pay data, only 14.4 percent implement formal strategies to close gaps.

Corporate Training Policies Supporting Career Mobility

Firms are increasingly adopting training policies, with global coverage rising to 96.8 percent in 2025.
