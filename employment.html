The Private Sector and Women’s Access to Quality Jobs

Tosin Gbadegesin · May 2026

Women’s economic participation has improved globally, yet the nature of work remains unequal. Women are more likely than men to be concentrated in lower-quality jobs—those with lower pay, limited security, and fewer advancement opportunities (World Bank 2024; ILO 2024). This raises an important question: what determines the quality of jobs women access?

Quality jobs are defined as employment that provides stable earnings, social protection, safe working conditions, and opportunities for skill development and career advancement (World Bank 2024; ILO 2024). This underscores that participation alone is not sufficient; the type of work women access is equally important. While governments shape laws and institutions, firms play a decisive role in determining how opportunities are distributed in practice. Understanding how labor markets, legal frameworks, and corporate behavior interact is therefore essential to closing the gap between participation and quality employment.

How Firms Shape Women’s Economic Opportunities

Women are working more than ever before. Across the world, female labor force participation has increased over time, supported by gains in education, declining fertility, and structural economic transformation (World Bank 2024; IMF 2023). However, greater participation has not consistently translated into better jobs.

In many economies, women remain concentrated in forms of work that fall short of quality job standards, including own-account work and unpaid family work, which often lack income security and formal protections (Fields 2011; ILO 2024). This concentration limits their access to stable earnings and career progression.

As a result, a persistent gap remains between having a job and having a good job, and closing this gap increasingly depends on firm-level decisions that shape hiring, job quality, and opportunities for advancement.

A World of Unequal Job Quality

Globally and across regions, women’s employment outcomes differ sharply. The data show that the share of women in vulnerable employment declined from about 44 percent in the early 1990s to around 37 percent in 2025. However, this progress masks large regional disparities. In Africa and South Asia, more than 60 percent of working women remain in vulnerable employment, while in Europe the share is closer to 13–14 percent.

These patterns align with broader evidence linking higher informality and lower economic development to greater reliance on vulnerable employment (ILO 2024; World Bank 2019).

Some regions have made progress, yet many women remain concentrated in work that offers limited income security and few pathways for advancement. The challenge is not only job creation but job quality.

Key Insight Women’s economic participation has increased globally, but job quality remains highly unequal across regions.

The Rules That Shape Opportunity

As women move into wage employment, laws help define what fair work looks like. Equal pay for work of equal value is a core protection. While many countries have adopted such legislation, coverage remains uneven, as shown in the data.

Evidence shows that stronger legal frameworks supporting women’s economic participation are associated with higher labor force participation and improved economic outcomes (World Bank 2024; Cuberes and Teignier 2016; IMF 2018).

Inside Firms: Where Women Work

Even within formal firms, women’s roles vary widely. The data show large differences across countries in women’s participation in production roles and overall employment. In the World Bank Enterprise Surveys, production workers refer to employees directly involved in producing goods or delivering services, including both skilled and unskilled workers, and excluding administrative or managerial staff (World Bank 2020).

In some contexts, women represent a majority of workers; in others, they remain underrepresented. These patterns align with evidence that women and men are unevenly distributed across occupations and sectors (World Bank 2012; ILO 2024).

Production roles often provide entry points into stable employment. Where women are underrepresented, access to these opportunities and their benefits is limited.

At the same time, differences in overall employment shares reflect hiring practices, workplace norms, and firm-level choices.

Together, these patterns underscore the central role firms play in shaping access to quality jobs.

Key Insight Who firms hire and for which roles strongly shape women’s access to stable, formal employment.

From Transparency to Accountability

Once women enter firms, the next question is whether they are treated equitably. The data shows that 45.3 percent of firms publish gender-disaggregated pay information, but only 14.4 percent have strategies to close gender pay gaps.

Research suggests that transparency can improve accountability (Bennedsen et al. 2022; World Bank 2024), but the data show that relatively few firms move from disclosure to action.

Regional variation reinforces this gap, some regions lead in transparency, others in action, but few achieve both. The result is a persistent disconnect between identifying inequality and addressing it.

Data Gap Most firms measure pay gaps, but far fewer implement strategies to close them.

Building Pathways to Better Jobs

Improving job quality also depends on whether workers can advance within firms. The chart below shows that most firms report policies ensuring equal access to training and career development. This signals strong recognition of the importance of skills.

Evidence links training to higher productivity, earnings growth, and mobility (Becker 1993; ILO 2024). However, policy adoption does not guarantee equal access.

Barriers such as time constraints, workplace norms, and unequal access can limit women’s ability to benefit from training opportunities (World Bank 2012).

Why It Matters Training policies can support upward mobility but only if women can access and benefit from them equally.

The Private Sector as a Driver of Change

Women’s employment outcomes are shaped by three interconnected forces: labor markets, legal frameworks, and firm behavior.

Labor markets determine where women work. Legal systems define the rules of participation. Firms determine how these rules are applied. They shape hiring, promotion, pay, and access to opportunity. Progress in one dimension alone is not enough.

Expanding stable employment matters. Strengthening legal protection matters. But without firm-level action through inclusive hiring, pay transparency, and investment in skills, these gains may not translate into better outcomes.

The private sector is not just part of the story. It is central to closing the gap between jobs and quality jobs and to ensuring that women’s work is not only more visible, but also more valued.
