From Jobs to Quality Jobs: Linking Vulnerable Employment, Pay Equity Laws, and Corporate Practices

Women's participation in labor markets has expanded globally, yet access to employment has not always translated into access to quality jobs. Many women remain concentrated in vulnerable forms of employment such as self-employment, own-account work, or contributing family work—where income stability, social protection, and opportunities for advancement are limited (World Bank 2012; ILO 2024). This analysis examines how labor market structures, equal pay laws, and corporate policies shape whether women's employment leads to equitable outcomes.

Persistent Gaps in Vulnerable Employment

Globally, about 37 percent of women were engaged in vulnerable employment in 2025, compared with about 44 percent in the early 1990s, indicating gradual progress over time. Although global levels have declined, large regional differences remain. In 2025, vulnerable employment among women remains above 60 percent in both Africa and South Asia, while it stands at roughly 13–14 percent in Europe. These disparities reflect differences in labor market structures and access to formal wage employment.

Several regions have made measurable progress over time. In Central Asia and Türkiye, the share of women in vulnerable employment declined from roughly 40 percent in the early 1990s to about 26 percent in 2025, while rates in the Middle East declined from around 32 percent to approximately 26 percent over the same period. Despite these improvements, many women remain concentrated in employment arrangements that lack stability and predictable earnings, highlighting the need to support transitions from vulnerable work into stable wage and salaried employment.

Legal Frameworks for Equal Pay and Economic Opportunity

As women move into wage employment, legal protections become critical for ensuring that job quality includes fair compensation and equal economic opportunity. Although many economies have adopted equal pay legislation, legal protections remain uneven across countries and regions. Several countries across Africa, Asia, and Latin America still lack explicit legal guarantees of equal remuneration for work of equal value.

Evidence shows that equality before the law and equal economic opportunity are not only social objectives but also key drivers of economic growth (Cuberes and Teignier 2016; World Bank 2024). Where legal protections are limited, women may face structural wage disparities even when participating in formal employment. In such contexts, corporate governance and workplace policies become particularly important in advancing pay equity in practice.

Gender Gaps Persist in Corporate Leadership

Corporate leadership diversity can influence workplace priorities and the adoption of gender-equitable policies. Globally, women hold 28.6 percent of board seats and account for 26.7 percent of senior management positions. Regional variation is significant. Europe records the highest representation of women on corporate boards at 39.5 percent, followed by Africa at 32.9 percent. In contrast, the Middle East reports only 7 percent of board seats held by women, highlighting persistent gender gaps in leadership positions.

Transparency and Action on Gender Pay Gaps

Corporate disclosure is an important step toward identifying and addressing gender pay disparities. Globally, 45.3 percent of firms disclose gender-disaggregated pay information, while only 14.4 percent publish formal strategies with at least two actions to close gender pay gaps—highlighting a gap between transparency and action.

Regional differences are pronounced. South Asia reports the highest disclosure rate at 95.8 percent, while Europe leads in implementing pay gap strategies at 36.8 percent. In contrast, only 1.2 percent of firms in the Middle East report such strategies, indicating that concrete policy action remains limited in many regions.

Corporate Training Policies Supporting Career Mobility

Beyond pay transparency, corporate policies that expand access to training and career development can help women transition into quality jobs. Globally, 93.1 percent of firms reported policies ensuring equal access to training in 2023, rising to 96.8 percent in 2025.

Regional trends follow a similar pattern. In Africa, adoption increased from 95.0 percent to 97.4 percent, while Europe rose from 94.1 percent to 97.9 percent over the same period. These high adoption rates suggest growing recognition among firms that equitable access to training supports workforce inclusion and upward mobility.

Aligning Labor Markets, Law, and Corporate Practice

The results highlight three interconnected dimensions shaping women's employment outcomes. Labor market structures continue to concentrate many women in vulnerable forms of work, particularly in regions where own-account and family employment remain widespread. Legal protections for equal remuneration remain uneven across countries, leaving gaps in wage equity frameworks. At the same time, corporate governance and workplace policies influence whether pay transparency, leadership diversity, and career development opportunities are implemented in practice.

Improving women's access to quality employment requires progress across labor markets, legal frameworks, and corporate practices. Expanding stable wage employment, strengthening equal pay legislation, and encouraging transparent pay practices and inclusive training policies can help ensure that women's labor force participation translates into equitable and sustainable economic opportunities.
